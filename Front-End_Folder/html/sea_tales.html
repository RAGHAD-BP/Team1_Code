Titanic

The largest passenger ship of its time hit an iceberg in the North Atlantic. It was called “unsinkable,” but sank in less than three hours. Due to high speed, ignoring ice warnings, and too few lifeboats, around 700 survived out of 2200. It became a symbol of human arrogance before the sea.

The Ghost Ship Mary Celeste

In 1872, the Mary Celeste was found floating with no crew on board. Everything was intact, but the crew vanished, leaving a mystery that fascinates the world.

The Bermuda Triangle

A mysterious area in the Atlantic Ocean, famous for the unexplained disappearance of ships and airplanes. Despite scientists’ attempts to explain the phenomenon, the incidents remain without clear answers. The Bermuda Triangle has become a symbol of mystery, reminding humanity of the secrets of the sea that cannot be easily unraveled.

The Lost Island of Sandy

Sailors reported a small island appearing during low tide. They found rare shells, but the island vanished with the tide, never to be seen again.

Kraken

Legends describe the Kraken as a giant sea monster with huge tentacles. Sailors feared it could sink ships, and many stories inspired modern tales of sea monsters.

Atlantis

A mythical city described as advanced and wealthy, but it sank into the sea because of the pride of its people. It has never been found, yet its story remained alive in books and legends. Atlantis became a symbol that no matter how powerful civilizations may become, they can still vanish before the force of nature.

Lusitania

It was the largest passenger ship of its time, sailing across the Atlantic during World War I. In 1915, it was struck by a German torpedo and sank in only 18 minutes. Out of nearly 2,000 passengers, fewer than a third survived. It became a symbol of the fragility of civilians in wartime and of the sea’s destructive power when combined with fire.

Mermaids

Ancient stories tell of beings half-human and half-fish, appearing to sailors amid the waves. Sometimes they sing with enchanting voices that lure ships toward the rocks, and sometimes they rescue the drowning. They became a symbol of mystery and beauty mixed with danger.

The Blue-Glowing Sea

People once wondered why the sea glowed bright blue at night. Fishermen said it was the ocean’s way of guiding them home. Only later did scientists explain that the glow comes from tiny creatures called bioluminescent plankton, lighting up whenever the waves move.

Devil’s Sea (Dragon’s Triangle)

Located in the Pacific Ocean near Japan, it became famous for mysterious disappearances of ships and fishermen. Named the “Dragon’s Triangle” because it resembles the Bermuda Triangle, where vessels and planes vanish without a trace. It stands as a symbol of maritime mystery that science has yet to clearly explain.

Costa Concordia

In 2012, a massive cruise ship carrying more than 4,000 people sailed near the coast of Italy. Due to a navigation error, it struck rocks and capsized on its side. It took many hours before the ship was fully evacuated, and most passengers survived. Yet the incident remained a lesson in the responsibility of leadership. It became a symbol that technology alone is never enough against a single human mistake.

Heracleion

Heracleion was once a major Egyptian port over two thousand years ago. It thrived with trade and temples until earthquakes and land collapse dragged the city into the water. It sank completely and remained lost until divers rediscovered it in 2000. A once-living city now rests quietly on the seafloor.
